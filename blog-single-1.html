    Monitoring Deforestation and Afforestation in the developing word:A remote sensing approach


    https://geo-gecko.github.io/forestryClassification/#11/2.9125/32.5139


    As the world seeks to slow the pace of climate change, preserve wildlife, and support billions increasing numbers of people, trees inevitably hold a major part of the answer. However, we’re losing 9.9 million acres of forests annually in Africa which is twice the world’s average deforestation rate. For example, according to FAO, forest cover in Uganda dropped from 24% to 9% of total land area between 1990 and 2015. The mass destruction of trees continues, sacrificing the long-term benefits of standing trees for short-term gain. In order to control and manage deforestation and degradation, quantitative information on forest activity is required for policy makers to make better informed decisions to protect forested areas. Remote sensing is one of the effective ways in which we have successfully monitored forest activity over time here at Geo Gecko for clients like International Institute for Tropical Agriculture, Danish Forestry Extension and Greater Virunga Transboundary Collaboration.

    Since the launch of the first Earth Resources Satellite Sensor (ERTS) in 1972, satellite remote sensing has provided advanced information on the structure of forested ecosystems. Satellite data derived information has improved over the years as a result of more sensors, improved classification algorithms, and the use of geographic information systems to incorporate additional spatially referenced data such as population density. The use of remote sensing provides three levels of information related to forest cover; that is the spatial extent, which can be used to assess the spatial dynamics of forest cover, the forest type and the biophysical and biochemical properties of forests. The assessment of forest information over time enables the comprehensive monitoring of forest resources.

    Using satellite data to classify and map various forest and land-use types is the most frequent use of satellite data. Pixels are classified according to their ground reflectance values as measured by the satellites. The desired map is created by displaying the classified pixels in their appropriate geographic context. The classes can be urban area, water, forest and vegetation. Using geographic information systems, the forests are extracted and the area being occupied by forests is calculated.

    Conversion of forests for urban or agricultural development, can also be detected and rates of change calculated by superimposing satellite images taken at different dates. Forestry questions regarding spatial variations, forest structure, and forest health pattern and the variables controlling observed patterns can be addressed using satellite imagery.
